Mills evaluates whether censoring misinformation transgresses democratic norms, works as intended, or is a good way of dealing with the issue. He contextualises the problem of misinformation through engaging the history of institutional trust and media.

Institutions, like universities, licensed professionals, and specialised government agencies act as epistemic authorities that produce and disseminate knowledge. I would also consider secondary education under this umbrella. Schools use indicators to measure student learning, the degree to which a student imbibes the prescribed curriculum. Schools are an interested institution to producing a disciplined, healthy, and educated population to undertake the the increasingly specialised labor of maintaining the state and living a good life. For example, singing the Pledge of Allegiance and opposing critical race theory serve to construct a nationlist imaginary in students, while physical education hopes to maintain their bodies. If education is instrumental, then we need to be careful to consider the degree to which it is credible. Schools in the US face censorship, regressive policymaking, and underfunding, which pose threats to the credibility and quality of the information that they disseminate.

I don't think censorship is justified unless there is direct harm invovled, like incitement to violence or doxxing. Otherwise, I believe that people should evaluate knowledge on their own terms. Censorship is deeply connected to power and can be easily used to weaponise and flatten difference. Truly democratic dissent should be invigorated by the exchange of ideas. The hope is that in a true democracy, people will be able to distinguish dubious and problematic claims from informed knowledge.
